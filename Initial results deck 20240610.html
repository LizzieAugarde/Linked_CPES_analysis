Linked CPES-registry data analysis - sexuality and language status

Lizzie Augarde

2024-10-06

Background and methods

The England National Cancer Patient Experience Survey (NCPES) allows cancer patients to give feedback on the care they have received. Results from the survey can be used to improve the experience of cancer patients at a national, regional and local level.

NCPES is delivered to a sample of patients who receive inpatient care for their cancer during April-June of each year. NDRS links NCPES response data to the cancer registry data. This allows for the analysis of clinical information for those completing the survey.

Linked NCPES-registry data is currently available for NCPES years 2017-2022. Each year, approximately 50,000 responses to NCPES are received, meaning there is a total of 254,504 patients in the cohort for this analysis.

The survey asks the following questions:

Q: Which of the following options best describes how you think of yourself? Answers: Heterosexual or Straight/ Gay or Lesbian / Bisexual / Other / Prefer not to say / Don’t know/not sure

Q: Is English your first language? Answers: Yes/No

Therefore, it is possible to use linked NCPES-registry data to analyse variation in clinical characteristics by sexuality and language status. These characteristics are not routinely recorded anywhere else in the cancer registry data, meaning this dataset offers a unique opportunity to identify any inequalities in outcomes by these characteristics. Routes to diagnosis

There is evidence of large variation in survival between those diagnosed via different routes. Exploring routes to diagnosis supports initiatives to promote earlier diagnosis of cancer and thereby improve survival rates and reduce cancer mortality. Differences in cancer outcomes between people with different sexualities and proficiency in English could be related to differences in how groups are likely to be diagnosed.

  • Language status and all routes

Research question: is the distribution of patients by route to diagnosis similar for non-native English-speaking patients, compared to native English speaking patients?

A higher proportion of respondents who are non-native English-speaking patients are diagnosed via emergency presentation, compared to native English-speaking patients. A lower proportion are diagnosed via screening or USC referrals. Otherwise, the distribution of respondents across the different routes to diagnosis is similar for native English-speaking and non-native English-speaking patients.

##Language status and emergency presentation

Research question: are non-native English-speaking patients more likely to be diagnosed via emergency presentation than native English speaking patients, accounting for other factors?

Logistic regression analysis of the association between reported language status and route to diagnosis, which accounted for patient age, deprivation, ethnicity, and tumour type, indicates there is no association between language status and likelihood of emergency presentation; non-native English-speaking patients are no more likely to be diagnosed via emergency presentation than English-speaking patients, accounting for these other factors.

Stage at diagnosis

Route to diagnosis is closely associated with stage at diagnosis, which is a key predictor of cancer mortality. Early stage at diagnosis is a key aim of the NHS Long-Term plan. As with routes to diagnosis, differences in cancer outcomes between people with different sexualities and proficiency in English could be related to how early different groups are likely to have their cancer diagnosed.

  • Sexuality
  • Language status

Research question: are the proportions of patients diagnosed at early and late stage similar for those patients who describe themselves as something other than heterosexual, compared to heterosexual patients?

Analysis of CPES responses linked to stage at diagnosis information from the NDRS cancer registry indicates that a lower proportion of respondents describing their sexuality as gay, lesbian, or other, are diagnosed at a later stage (stage 3 or 4), compared to heterosexual respondents.

Research question: are patients who describe themselves as something other than heterosexual more likely to be diagnosed at late stage than heterosexual patients, accounting for other factors?

Logistic regression analysis of the association between reported sexuality and stage at diagnosis, which accounted for patient age, deprivation, ethnicity, and tumour type, indicates there is no association between sexuality and stage at diagnosis; patients describing themselves as something other than heterosexual are no more likely to be diagnosed at late stage than heterosexual patients, accounting for these other factors.

Research question: are the proportions of patients diagnosed at early and late stage similar for non-native English speaking patients, compared to native English speaking patients?

Analysis of CPES responses linked to stage at diagnosis information from the NDRS cancer registry indicates that respondents describing themselves as non-native English speakers were less likely to be diagnosed at a later stage than native English speaking respondents.

Research question: are non-native English-speaking patients more likely to be diagnosed at late stage than native English-speaking patients, accounting for other factors?

Logistic regression analysis of the association between reported language status and stage at diagnosis, which accounted for patient age, deprivation, ethnicity, and tumour type, indicates there is an association between language status and stage at diagnosis. The odds of non-native English-speaking patients being diagnosed late are 0.89 times the odds of English-speaking patients being diagnosed late. This means the adjusted likelihood of late diagnosis for non-English speaking patients is 47.2%, compared to approximately 52.8% for English-speaking patients.

Kaplan-Meier survival analysis indicates there is no difference between the probability of survival at least 1 year (log rank test chi-squared statistic = 1.9, p = 0.2) post-diagnosis, between those respondents describing themselves as something other than heterosexual, and heterosexual respondents.

At 5 years post-diagnosis, there is evidence of a difference in survival probability by sexuality (log rank test chi-squared statistic 4.5, p <0.05). The heterosexual group experiences a higher observed number of deaths compared to what would be expected if survival were the same across both sexuality groups.

However, after accounting for age, ethnicity, cancer type, deprivation and stage at diagnosis, this association is no longer statistically meaningful (p = 0.8).

Conclusions

This analysis indicates that, after accounting for confounding factors, there is no evidence of statistically meaningful associations between NCPES respondents’ sexuality and and routes to diagnosis, stage to diagnosis, or survival. There is also no evidence of statistically meaningful associations between NCPES respondents’ language status and and routes to diagnosis or survival.

There is some evidence of an association between language status and stage at diagnosis. After accounting for confounders, non-native English-speaking respondents are slightly less likely than English-speaking respondents to be diagnosed at a late stage.

This analysis suggests there is little inequality in diagnostic characteristics and survival by sexuality or language status, which is not accounted for by the demographic composition of these groups.
